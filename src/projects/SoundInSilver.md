Sound In Silver is a zine and 16mm film compilation, compiled for NIME'26, as an alt.NIME submission. The alt.NIME track is a new addition to the international conference for new interfaces for musical expression. It comes in reaction to the discontinuation of alt.chi, "a forum for controversial, risk-taking, and boundary-pushing research” at CHI, the conference for Human Factors in Computing Systems. This track promotes unconventional contributions, that choose an alternative format, submission and presentation, worrying at the boundaries of what NIME is and can be.

![Image of zine](imgs/6c6ab8f7-db0b-4cdb-8bf4-f9aa3a33b83f2.JPG)
Image of risographed zine

This work was a collaboration between myself, Graeme Hogg, Jennifer Wicks, Kathy Hinde, Matt Dacies, Melanie Clifford, Vicky Smith, Lewis Heriz and Enrique del Castillo.

The intention of this collaboratively made zine and screening was to share the works of the experimental film community, through distribution of printed works as well as in-person experiences of 16mm optical sound films. The zines and film were primarily made for NIME 2026, but the zines are to be further distributed among individual artist filmmakers, collectives and film labs. The film will be screened at future events facilitated by the photochemical experimental film community. The body of work collates 16mm film strips and details of tools and processes from a collection of optical sound
filmmakers. Sharing a small corner of a much wider world of contemporary practice, we present photochemical optical
sound not as a heritage medium, but as a continually shifting material sound system shaped through slow process, touch and material inscription.

![Image of zine](imgs/41465938-0ab0-41d9-bfa4-52971358160e2.JPG)

Enrique Del Castillo and Graeme Hogg contributed to the zine only, although Graeme Hogg made prints of certain contributions using his Steenbeck-turned-contact printer. Every other contributor submitted work to both zine and the 16mm film collage.

The zine is accessible in a larger format [here](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1FV_UIvSz1iEjmZZDp4SpFV8pHB-0xFKp) and embedded within the pages below. Images and text were printed, hand cut, arranged and cut by myself and Dave Howell.  

A film of the 16mm screening installation can be found [here](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1dOkEFFHdNeBKvTL8VEf9QvguRW4zAmdQ).
Another film with different examples of the author's work, further illuminating optical sound practices can be found [here](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1-GlqyrkMXggyoqZnTn11fK7J6PBizJrI).

NIME 16mm film screening installation

The film comprises of 7 excerpts from the following works: 

**Lewis Heriz** - a section of Bentwater's Frequency Test. "Sound was contact-printed on a converted Steenbeck (editing table) using loops made from found footage, and developed. Only the optical sound strip was fixed, in order to then contact print the image area using a hand torch without destroying the printed sound in the process." 

**Jennifer Wicks** - an excerpt of *GENPUR 7.1 computer music*, photograms of 1970s mainframe tape.

**Kathy Hinde** - a selection of photograms of broken glass (from car break ins, part of a collaboration with artist Harriet Bowman), river sediment and algae (from the river Frome, as part of the River Traces project), and leaf skeletons (preserved by Kathy's Gran in Wigan in the 1960s). Each photogram creates animated shifting patterns and different forms and tones of noise.

**Jasmine Butt** - a section of animated forms, using a frame by frame printing method, using Pure Data, an enlarger and a mobile phone. The forms are animated and morphed scans from *Optical Designs in Motion with Moirée Overlays*, a 1976 book by Carol Belanger Grafton, found in a charity shop on East Street, Bedminster in Bristol.

**Matt Davies and Melanie Clifford** - an excerpt from New Brunswick Swing, a silent section of flicker film, which Matt and Melanie use for an expanded installation, where photocell pendulums swing in front of a projection of beating patterns. *"Turning the projector into a camera, replacing the halogen bulb with sound-to-light module, we exposed the raw film stock to rhythmic pulses of light. Creating a silhouette of the film gate with variable densities as the new bulb fades in and out."*. The second excerpt donated by Matt and Melanie was a film outcome from an optical sound hackathon, where a CRT machine was filmed with a super16mm camera. Video synthesis spills into the soundtrack, creating purring 24 fps rumble with the frame lines, as well as roaring, pulsing noisy tones and piercing high frequencies. 

**Vicky Smith** - a printed excerpt from NOISY LICKING, DRIBBLING AND SPITTING, made in 2014. Vicky used dyed spit and structured tongue prints to create a film exploring the human body, excitation and climax. *"The sonic impression of bodily fluid optical sound films is of a swooshing, crackling and popping, which can either be undifferentiated, or structured with pauses and measure... In bodily fluid films, the predominant sensation is that of noise."*

We thank Dave Howell (Six of Swords), 16 Tonne Press, Nachleben Film Lab, UWE's CT Lab, BEEF, Lew Gardiner, David Leicester and Rachel Deakin for their help in producing and installing this work.
