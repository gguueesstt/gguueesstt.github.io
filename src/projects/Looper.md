James Holcombe of Erehwon Film once told me that someone he knows made a working film looper out of a 7 inch record and a carrot. After I heard this, I thought it can't be so difficult!! (later found out it was Sally Golding of Other Film who made the carrot looper)

![Looper image1](/imgs/IMG_4906.jpeg)

So I have made my own looper, which I have used regularly for about 8 years or so. 
It is basically a wooden lazy susan I found in the hack rack at Bristol Hackspace. I glued a plastic film core to the middle of the spinning plate. 
I then drilled holes in the corners of the base plate and an identical shape of wood. I use this to clamp the looper on to the projector handle, with bolts and butterfly nuts. 
All I do then is use a mini bosshead clamp to hold a paintbrush (could be anything long and smoothish) over the film reel to keep it down, in place. 

It is very easy to make your own, you just have to get a lazy susan bearing, a circular piece of wood, two rectangular pieces, a film core and a few more bolts, nuts and bits and pieces. It takes a while to learn how to actually wind and thread and easily splice the loop, but with a bit of practice it works and costs you very little. I love being able to loop 100ft films for projecting visuals at gigs. It makes a nice change from short 10ft loops, giving you a few minutes of material to work with. 

Tips: 
When looping up your film, try to find a core or something that is wider than the core that is stuck on the looper. This way you can just easily slide it on there. You want at least a finger's amount of space between the loop of film, and the core of the looper, to feed out your film from the middle of the loop. 
Make sure that your reels aren't scratching (ideally use daylight loading reels) and that projector arms are at the right height. You want the front arm taking up the feed out to be higher than the looper so that the film can come up and out of the centre, and the arm feeding the film back into the loop to be just level so that the film doesn't slip off or interfere with the feed out.
